Monday June 15

Stellar Evolution
12:00 - 12:30Stan Woosley The Evolution and Explosion of Mass-Losing Helium Stars
In a series of four papers our group has explored the evolution of stars in sufficiently tight binaries that the hydrogen envelope is lost at the onset of helium burning. Subsequent mass loss by winds is included. The results resemble those of single stars except that presupernova core mass for a given main sequence mass is appreciably smaller. Our model stars are exploded using a one-dimensional treatment of neutrino-transport calibrated to SN 1987A and the Crab. Explosion energies, nucleosynthesis, and remnant properties are thus sensitive to presupernova structure. The mass distributions for neutron stars and black holes are in good agreement with measured properties. No black hole mass gap is found at low masses, though there is a reduced probability below 5 solar masses. Our predictions will be useful to LIGO and show the possibility of measuring the stellar IMF for high mass stars. The light curves of the Type Ib and Ic supernovae these explosions produce have been simulated in the KEPLER and SEDONA codes.Most have presupernova masses between 2.7 and 5.6 solar masses. No explosion produces a $^{56}$Ni mass greater than 0.15 solar masses, a bolometric luminosity over 10**(42.5) erg/s, or a visual magnitude brighter than -18.0. These limits agree with many but not all observations of Ib and Ic supernovae suggesting that many allegedly normal Type Ib and Ic supernovae may not be illuminated at peak by radioactive decay.
12:30 - 1:00Chloe Sandoval Multidimensional Late Stage Stellar Evolution of a 15 M⊙ Star
Core-collapse supernovae (CCSNe) and their progenitors are some of the most astrophysically interesting objects in the universe. We have done a multidimensional study of the late stage stellar evolution, silicon shell burning (SiSB), that directly proceeds core-collapse of a 15 M⊙ star. SiSB is highly convective and creates asymmetries in CCSNe progenitors that affect stellar structure, nucleosynthesis, and the growth and collapse of the iron core. We have simulated a 15 M⊙ star from stellar birth to SiSB in 1D, using Modules for Experiments in Stellar Astrophysics (MESA) and SiSB to Iron core collapse in 1D and 2D using the MESA progenitor and the Polaris code.
1:00 - 1:30Chris Fryer Opportunities for TEAMS: Ties to Experiments and Observations
A broad set of new opportunities in experiments and observations are being proposed that require input from the modeling efforts led by this SciDAC in supernovae and gamma-ray bursts. We are in an ideal time to develop ties to those programs. I will review those programs and start a discussion on how we might tie better with those programs.

Methods I
1:45 - 2:15Andy Nonaka AMReX-Astro Updates - Development and Science
We provide an overview of recent developments from the AMReX-Astro code suite, consisting of CASTRO, MAESTROeX, and the shared Microphysics repository. The primary developments have been related to full-GPU conversion using C++ macros and CUDA extended lambdas. New scientific studies have recently begun including X-ray bursts, merging white dwarfs, stellar convection with rotation, electron capture supernovae, and the convective URCA process.
2:15 - 2:45Andrew Christlieb Highly efficient high order kinetic transport model
We develop a highly efficient high order method for the Gray equation of transport in 5D. The basic method is based on a split step approach, where the model is split into a collided and un-collided species. The steps of the method are to: (1) solve the un-collided species with fast sweeping, (2) move the particles the collided from the un-collided species into the collided species, (3) use a fully implicit update to solve the collided species. At the end of the step, the collided species are re-promoted into the un-collided species. The discretization is based on Discontinuous Galerkin. Efficiency gains are made by using a low number of quadrature angles for the collided species and a full set of quadrature angles for the un-collided species. A conservative map that ensures positivity based Nystrom reconstruction is used to move between the two resolutions. The base idea is that using a low number of quadrature angles in the collided model is sufficient for capturing highly collisional regions, and that using a full set of quadrature angles in the un-collided model will capture the free streaming regions. The whole algorithm is rapped in a "space time" Integral Differed Correction framework that corrects for the errors made in both splitting and in the errors introduced by using a low number of angles quadrature angles in the fully implicit collided update. The method is the first splitting algorithm of this type to be able to correctly described the transition from a highly collisional region to a free streaming region while maintaining high order in space and time. In tests we show that the method is two orders of magnitude faster than the fully resolved high order method with the same computational accuracy. This method will serve as the basses for our new work we are developing on using ML for closure models in M1 transport. We will briefly outline our planned work in this area.
2:45 - 3:15Sean Couch Development of high-order MHD solvers for astrophysical applications
Supernovae, massive stellar interiors, and neutron star mergers are fundamentally turbulent, magnetohydrodynamic systems. As such, accurately simulating these phenomena requires numerical solvers that are capable of capturing both the impact of magnetic fields and the difficult-to-resolve turbulence. High-order accurate MHD solvers present attractive features for tackling these types of problems. I will discuss our efforts to implement high-order MHD solvers in FLASH using both finite volume and finite difference formulations. I will discuss initial performance and relative strengths and weaknesses of each method for addressing astrophysical simulations.

Explosions I
3:30 - 4:00Adam Burrows What Multi-D Simulation Models Reveal About CCSN Explosion Systematics
I will explore the mapping between progenitor structure and CCSN explosion observables, as revealed by state-of-the-art multi-D core-collapse simulations.
4:00 - 4:30Jonah Miller Neutrino Transport and Nucleosynthesis in Collapsar Disks
When a very massive, rapidly rotating star runs out of nuclear fuel and undergoes core-collapse, a black hole-accretion disk system may form. In these so-called "failed supernovae," accretion onto the rotating black hole can power a jet of relativistic material out the poles. If this jet is sufficiently powerful to disrupt the in-falling stellar envelope, the system explodes as a "jet-driven supernova" or "collapsar." We discuss the recently revived suggestion that collapsars are a major source of the fusion of heavy elements in the universe. We perform general relativistic neutrino radiation magnetohydrodynamics simulations of collapsar disks and extract nucleosynthetic yields. We show how accurate treatment of neutrino transport in the disk transport and the inclusion in-falling stellar material is required to accurately capture the dynamics. We find that very few heavy elements are produced, providing counter-evidence to the aforementioned suggestion.
4:30 - 5:00David Vartanyan The Landscape of Supernovae Observations from Detailed Simulations
I will present observational insight into gravitational wave and neutrino signatures from detailed three-dimensional core-collapse supernovae simulations spanning a broad suite of progenitor masses. Generally, such events are observables for galactic events with current and next-generation detectors. Simultaneous detection of both will explore the large- and small-scale physical motions of the exploder, provide insight into the physics of the forming neutrino star, and illuminate the mechanism of explosion. Additionally, we provide some of the first estimates of gravitational waveforms from neutrino asymmetries in addition to matter motions. Synergistic detection of gravitational waves and neutrinos will provide physical information about supernovae evolution otherwise opaque to our efforts.

Tuesday June 16

Explosions II
12:00 - 12:30Trevor Sprouse Following fission products in explosive astrophysical environments
Explosive, neutron-rich astrophysical events are thought to produce the heaviest elements found in nature via the rapid neutron capture (r) process of nucleosynthesis. In sufficiently neutron-rich r-process conditions, nuclear fission processes can become extremely active. As such, robust theoretical predictions of fission product yields represent one of several critical steps towards fully understanding the astrophysical production of the heaviest elements. We present a new technique for quantitatively following fission products throughout r-process nucleosynthesis calculations, and we apply this technique in order to identify the relative contributions of individual fission product yields to overall r-process nucleosynthesis.
12:30 - 1:00Luke Johns Neutrino oscillations in supernovae and mergers
It remains an open question how neutrino oscillations affect the dynamics and astronomical observables of core-collapse supernovae and neutron-star mergers. Flavor evolution in these environments is nonlinear and multi-scale, and the challenge is figuring out how to make it computable. I’ll summarize recent advances and offer my perspective on how further progress might be made. The (dis)similarities to other transport problems will be heavily emphasized.

Methods II
1:15 - 1:45Michael Zingale Overview of Spectral Deferred Correction Methods
The AMReX Astrophysics codes MAESTROeX and Castro have several different time integration methods based on spectral deferred corrections which aim to improve the coupling between hydrodynamics and reactions. We will discuss the core ideas behind these methods and show some test problems and early science results.
1:45 - 2:15Platon Karpov Sapsan: Framework for Supernovae Turbulence Modeling with Machine Learning
Turbulence plays a significant role in many different phenomena, including Core-Collapse Supernovae (CCSN). Unfortunately, current state-of-the-art simulations have to resort to using subgrid models for turbulence treatment, as direct numerical simulations (DNS) are too expensive to run. However, said subgrid models, such as large eddy simulation (LES), lack accuracy when compared to DNS results. Recently, there has been a new trend of utilizing Machine Learning (ML) techniques, with impressive prediction capability for the turbulence closure. That being said, the domain science field in question lacks the expertise to employ ML for their respective projects, not to say for turbulence treatment. We have developed Sapsan, a pipeline designed to simplify ML implementation, focusing on physics-informed machine learning and analytical tools to aid our team and researchers in general to study turbulence in supernovae and other astrophysical environments. Sapsan incorporates data pre-processing, model construction, experiment tracking, GUI & CLI interfaces, and can be used to facilitate a variety of scientific machine learning tasks.
2:15 - 2:45Mike Pajkos Incorporating GRMHD into the FLASH Framework
Because of the variety of physics involved in proper numerical models of core collapse supernovae (CCSNe), they stand as one of the main scientific drivers for improving HPC resources. Motivated by the need to provide reliable gravitational wave predictions for the next Galactic CCSNe, I will review the newest piece of physics within the FLASH framework—relativistic MHD. Likewise I will outline the future developments for FLASH with respect to physics and performance portability.
